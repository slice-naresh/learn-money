🧒 Tax, simply

When you earn a lot — or your money grows a lot — the government takes a small bite called tax. Some piggy-bank types like PPF have no bite at all. Shares have a small bite. Crypto has a big bite. Tax is why "what you keep" is less than "what it grew to."

Income tax slabs

A "slab" means different parts of your income are taxed at different rates — only the income inside each band pays that band's rate. You pick one of two regimes each year.

Surcharge & cess

On top of the slab tax, very high incomes pay an extra surcharge, and everyone pays a 4% health & education cess on the tax amount.
  • Income up to ₹50L — no surcharge
  • ₹50L–₹1Cr — 10% surcharge
  • ₹1Cr–₹2Cr — 15% surcharge
  • Above ₹2Cr — 25% surcharge
  • Then 4% cess on the total tax (surcharge for capital gains is capped at 15%).

Deductions (old regime only)

Deductions lower the income you're taxed on. The old regime trades higher rates for these:
  • 80C — up to ₹1.5L: PPF, ELSS, EPF, SSY, SCSS, ULIP, life insurance, home-loan principal. (products marked 80C in Investing earn this)
  • 80CCD(1B) — up to ₹50k: extra NPS contribution, on top of 80C.
  • 80D: health insurance premiums (self + family + parents).
  • 80TTA / 80TTB: savings-account / senior-citizen interest.
  • Section 24(b) — up to ₹2L: interest paid on a home loan.

🧒 Credit cards, simply

A credit card lets you spend the bank's money for a few weeks free. If you pay it all back on time → it stays free. If you pay late or only the tiny "minimum", the bank charges a LOT extra every month (interest) and the debt grows. Rule: only spend what you can pay back fully. Try the calculator below to see the trap.

1. How the billing cycle works

A credit card is a short-term loan you take every time you swipe. The bank groups it into a monthly cycle:
  • Statement / billing date: the bank totals everything you spent that cycle into one bill.
  • Due date: usually ~15–20 days after the statement. This gap is the grace period.
  • Grace period (interest-free): pay the full statement amount by the due date and you pay ₹0 interest — you used the bank's money free for up to ~45–50 days.
  • Total due vs. minimum due: the bill shows both. "Minimum due" (~5% of the balance) is the smallest payment to avoid a late fee — it is not the smart amount to pay.

2. Paying on time vs. late — this is everything

✅ Pay full amount, on time

  • ₹0 interest — grace period stays intact.
  • You keep your rewards/cashback for free.
  • Credit score rises — on-time payment is the biggest score factor.
  • The card works for you: convenience + a free short loan.

⚠️ Pay late, partial, or only the minimum

  • Grace period is lost — interest (36–48%/yr) is charged from the purchase date, not the due date.
  • Interest applies to the old balance AND every new spend until you clear it fully.
  • Late-payment fee (tiered, ~₹500–₹1,300) + 18% GST on fees and interest.
  • Credit score drops; 30+ days late is reported and can hurt for years.
  • Repeated defaults → card blocked, higher rates, collection calls.
The golden rule: always pay the full statement amount by the due date. If you ever can't, pay as much above the minimum as possible — the minimum is a debt trap, not a target.

3. How interest is actually charged

Once you miss a full payment and start "revolving" a balance:
  • Interest is quoted monthly (e.g. 3.5%/month) which is about 42%/year — far higher than any loan.
  • It's charged daily on your running balance from each purchase date, so it compounds fast.
  • New purchases get no grace period either, until the full balance is cleared.
Example: ₹50,000 unpaid at 42%/yr is about ₹1,750 of interest in the very first month — before you buy anything new.

4. Other costly traps

🏧
Cash withdrawal. Taking cash on a card charges interest from day one (no grace) plus a 2.5–3% fee. Avoid unless an emergency.
📉
Over-limit fee. Spending past your limit triggers a fee and a score hit.
🧾
EMI conversion. "No-cost EMI" usually isn't — interest is often hidden in the price or a processing fee. Read the rate.
🌐
Forex markup. Overseas/foreign-currency spends add a ~1.5–3.5% markup.

5. Rewards vs. real cost

Cashback, points and miles are genuinely valuable — but only on one condition.
  • Rewards are typically worth ~1–2% of what you spend.
  • Card interest is ~42%/year. One month of interest wipes out a whole year of rewards.
  • So rewards are free money only if you pay in full every month. Otherwise the card is winning, not you.

6. Your credit score (CIBIL)

A 300–900 score lenders use to decide your loans and rates. A card is the easiest way to build it — or wreck it.
  • On-time payments (~35%) — the single biggest factor. Never miss a due date.
  • Credit utilisation (~30%) — keep spends under ~30% of your limit; high usage looks risky.
  • Age of credit, credit mix, new enquiries make up the rest — don't churn many cards/loans at once.
  • A good score (750+) later gets you cheaper home/car loans — that's the real long-term payoff.
